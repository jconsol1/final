Construction, Congestion, and Quality of Life in College Park

By: James Consoli
With housing developments arising, new rails of transportation in the works, and roadway changes being implemented in the local area, the city of College Park is urbanizing and thriving like never before.

From College Park to Construction Park

Since 2020, a series of housing development requests have been approved and put into action across the city.

“Developers are practically beating our doors down with new student housing projects.”
- College Park Mayor, Patrick Wojahn.



According to Wojahn, the city is bringing these developments, in-part, to bring more student residents to the city. However, non-student residents are a priority as well, said Wojahn.

“It is critical to encourage more non-student residents to live in College Park, to ensure that our downtown commercial areas do well even in the summer when not as many students are around,” Wojahn said. “One thing I think College Park has struggled with over the years is that historically it’s been more of a commuter campus. People will live all around the area and commute to go to school, to go to class or to teach or do research, and then [they] disappear in the evening.”

With this in mind, it is important to recognize the University of Maryland’s presence as it relates to the city’s progression. More than 40,000 students currently attend Maryland, according to the university’s division of research. Not only does this surplus of students play a role in the city’s approval of housing grants, but also when it comes to creating affordable housing.

The city is ranked eighth amongst cities in Maryland in terms of population size, but third from last in terms of median income, according to the United States Census Bureau.

Therefore, creating affordable housing for its residents is a project the city continues to prioritize.

“The city is always concerned to have affordable housing, housing for grad students, and housing for undergrad students,” said Michael Williams, the economic development manager for the city. “That’s always a priority for us.”

Creating Options for Commute away from Cars

More than 30,000 vehicles drive in or through College Park daily, according to the Maryland Department of Transportation.

Due in part to this volume, the city and county have attempted to alleviate congestion from cars.

To date, discussions in College Park involving commuting often center around environmentally friendly transportation and lowering carbon emissions.

City residents and local commuters now await an expansive railway transit that will check both of those boxes.

The Purple Line is an electronically sustainable, 16-mile light railway system that will connect more of the region's railways, while connecting more people to jobs.

In terms of economic and environmental impact, it is estimated to take 17,000 cars off the road daily, ultimately saving one million gallons of gas within the next 20 years, according to MDOT. The line is estimated to be completed by 2026.

On a local level, College Park’s City Council has organized projects that hope to inspire less vehicular traffic too.

Daily construction on Baltimore Avenue, in College Park, is part of a two-phase plan which will bring new medians to separate lanes going in both directions, eliminate suicide lanes, add new pedestrian lighting, and implement other roadway changes. The project is set to be completed in the summer of 2023, according to MDOT.

Another notable change, demanded by local residents for decades, includes the creation of a bike lane on Baltimore Avenue. This idea has been in the works since the 1980s, yet this is the first time the strip is painted on the roadway.

This lane is also a part of the overall plan to alleviate car use in the city and instead incentivize more pedestrian and cyclist traffic.

“One of the goals that we have overall is to make it so that people don’t feel the need to use their cars as much,” said Wojahn. “So, putting more amenities within walking or biking distance, making it safer to walk and bike—those are all part of that strategy.”

Infrastructure Capabilities and Population Forecasts

Something important to recognize given all of this is that College Park is urbanizing at a rapid rate. The mayor expects the population to leap another 5,000, from approximately 35,000 to 40,000 in the next five years.

For context, the population of the city was about 25,000 in 2007, when the mayor first joined the council. From 2007 to roughly five years from now, the city expects a 60 percent increase in population.

That said, getting around College Park will not be the same.

The city may be in the process of creating new developments for transportation and adapting the infrastructure to fit people’s interests. Yet, a concern worth considering is whether the city will be able to add the residents and ensure a similar, or a better quality of life.

According to recent U.S. census findings, College Park is growing faster than approximately 72% of other similarly sized cities since the year 2000.

Recent data shows the city is one of the fastest growing cities in the state.
While the city, county, and state have worked years and years on transportation/infrastructure projects, construction of these projects across the city and state are still ways away from being completed.

The two-phase roadway plan on Baltimore Avenue is still months from being finished, and the Purple Line is still years from completion.

Couple this with the incoming residents signing yearly leases in the multiple new housing developments, and the city will have no issues of people living in it year- round.

Instead, it may face new issues of too many people and too much activity, without sufficient infrastructure and public transit present to support its growing population.
